Charles Alfred Angot

Born on 4th July 1848 in Paris. Died in Paris on 16th March 1924.
Angot studied physics and mathematics at the École Normale Supérieure (1868-1872). After his degree, he briefly worked as assistant at the Collège de France (1872-1874). He then took part in astronomic expeditions in New Caledonia (1874) and the United States (1878). These international travels enabled him to visit astronomical and meteorological observatories in the United States facilitating his following career in meteorology.
In 1879 Angot was appointed “météorologiste titulaire” at the French Central Meteorological Bureau in Paris, where he worked during all his life, eventually becoming its third director in 1907. He retired from the Bureau in 1920, when the French geophysical services were reorganised. Angot’s scientific research spanned meteorological observations and meteorological instruments, the study of climatology, solar radiation, geomagnetic and astronomic phenomena. With his directorship of the French Meteorological Bureau, Angot also became the representative of the French meteorological services in the International Meteorological Organization.
Alongside his career within the meteorological Bureau, since 1879 Angot was engaged in teaching physics and meteorology at the French Institut National Agronomique, making him an ideal candidate for the presidency of the IMO Commission for Agricultural Meteorology. Due to his interest in the application of meteorology to agriculture, Angot was also a member of the French Académie d’Agriculture.
Angot’s scientific research was mainly published in the Annales du Bureau Central Météorologique, the official publication of the French meteorological services. He also published a few meteorological textbooks that went through several editions.
In his time Angot was a notable member of scientific circles in France and abroad. He was an honorary member of the Royal Meteorological Society, of the Royal Society of Edinburgh, of the Swedish Royal Academy of Sciences, of the Geographical Society of Roumania.

Cornelis Braak

Born on 12th January 1880 in Oudorp, The Netherlands. Died on 23rd March 1973 in the Netherlands.
Braak obtained his doctorate at Leiden University in 1908 and there remained as assistant for a few months before taking up an appointment at the Dutch Magnetic and Meteorological Observatory in Batavia in August 1908. Braak spent many years in Java studying the climate of the Dutch East Indies and in 1920 he became the director of the Batavia observatory.Five years later, in 1925, he was offered the directorship of the climatology department of the KNMI in de Bilt. He maintained this position until his retirement at the end of 1947. During his forty-years career in meteorology, Braak focused mainly on climatology both in the Dutch colonies and in the Netherlands, publishing several contributions on the topic. During the 1930s and 1940s, his department also strengthened research work in agricultural meteorology examining the influence of weather on crops. Braak gained an international profile as a member of the International Meteorological Organization. He was at first secretary and then president of the IMO Commission for Agricultural Meteorology.

Rudolf Feige

Born on 28th January 1889 in Breslau (now Wrocław, Poland). Died in Jerusalem on 25th May 1948.
Feige studied physics and mathematics at the University of Breslau. In August 1919 he became the director of the Meteorological Observatory Breslau-Krietern and from 1923 he was also in charge of the weather service in Breslau-Krietern. He moved to Palestine around 1936 and became head of the Aviation Forecasting Service in 1937, when Palestine was still a British mandate. He died in 1948 in Jerusalem during an Arab bomb attack.

Rudolf Geiger

Born on 24th August 1894 in Erlangen, Germany. Died in Munich on 22nd January 1981.
Geiger studied physics and mathematics at the universities of Erlangen and Kiel from 1912 to 1915 and gained his doctorate in Erlangen in 1920. Completed his studies, he became an assistant at the Physics Institute of the Technical University Darmstadt and there he remained until 1923, when he joined the newly founded Meteorological and Climatological Institute at the University of Munich. In Munich he became also an observer for the Bavarian Weather Service (1928-1935). During his time in Munich, Geiger began the researches on microclimatology that he continued throughout his life. In 1937 he moved to the Forestry Academy in Eberswalde as professor of meteorology, mathematics and physics. He remained in Eberswalde for a decade before returning to Munich in 1948 as professor of meteorology, climatology and microclimatology and director of the university meteorological institute. He retired in 1959. Geiger’s research on microclimatology brought him to focus on agrometeorological and forestry problems, such as frost damage and frost protection, because soil conditions and plant growth are key element in the microclimate of an area. By the time he became secretary and then president of the IMO Commission for Agricultural Meteorology in the 1930s he had already produced a wealth of publications on these topics and continued until his retirement. As part of his climatological research, Geiger contributed also to the climate classification system developed by the meteorologist Wladimir Köppen, which is widely used nowadays.

Pierre Rey

Born in 1876. Died in 1946 in Paris.
Rey began his studies at the agronomy school in Montpellier in 1896. He remained affiliated to this institution for several years working on climatological and weather forecasting problems connected to agriculture, and eventually became the director of the local meteorological station. After WWI, he became a general inspector of the newly created French Agronomy Institute (IRA) and at same time he was appointed director of the 'Station Centrale de Physique Agricole', based in Versailles, where he continued his weather and climate research. When the IRA was dismantled in the 1930s, Rey continued to work in agronomic research with different roles, until he was suspended from duties by the Vichy regime and reinstated only posthumously. Rey was a long-term member of the IMO Commission for Agricultural Meteorology, where he served from 1919 until 1937.

Wilhelm Mathäus Schmidt

Born on 21st January 1883 in Vienna, Austria. Died in Vienna on 27th November 1936.
Schmidt studied physics and mathematics at the university of Vienna and gained there his doctorate in 1905. In the same year, he began to work at the Central Office for Meteorology and Geodynamics in Vienna where he remained until his appointment as professor of meteorology and climatology at the Vienna Hochschule für Bodenkultur in 1919. He concluded his professional career as professor of geophysics at the university of Vienna and director of the Central Office for Meteorology and Geodynamics. Schmidt’s main contributions to agricultural meteorology are his microclimatological studies aimed at understanding how temperature, humidity, and solar radiation are interconnected. Due to his scientific interests, he became a member of the subcommission on microclimatology instituted by the IMO Commission on Agricultural Meteorology. Schmidt also contributed to study frost hazards and frost protection to support Austrian farmers.

Victor Vasilyevich Sinelshikov

Born in 1909. Died in Obninsk on 26th May 1968.
Sinelshikov’s held several directive positions in the USSR Hydrometeorological Service. When he died, he was deputy director of the Institute of Experimental Meteorology in Obninsk, where he was in charge of the agrometeorological study of field experiments.
Sinelshikov was well-known in the international community of agricultural meteorology. He was president of the IMO Commission for Agricultural Meteorology from 1946 until 1951 and in 1953 became the USSR representative in the WMO Commission for Agricultural Meteorology. He was elected vice-president of this second Commission in 1967. Sinelshikov was also a member of the editorial board of the journal Agricultural Meteorology, founded in 1964.

Axel Wilhelm Wallén

Born on 27th July 1877 in Göteborg. Died in Stockholm on 24th February 1935.
Wallén graduated from the University of Göteborg in 1905. In 1906 he gained his doctorate from the University of Uppsala, where he taught also hydrography. In 1907 Wallén became president of the newly established Swedish Hydrographic Bureau. When the Hydrographic Bureau merged with the Swedish meteorological services in 1918, he became director of the new institution, the Statens meteorologisk-hydrografiska anstalt, where he continued to work until his death in 1935.
Wallén scientific research focused on the study of the Swedish water system and climate. As director of the hydrographic and meteorological services, he worked on the forecasting of floods and storms and the periodicity of climate. Investigations in agricultural meteorology became a concern of Wallén since the mid-1910s, when he began to publish on the impact of temperatures on harvests and forests. After WWI, he became a member of the IMO Commission for Agricultural Meteorology, where he acted as secretary at first and then as president until his sudden death. Wallén was a member of the Swedish Academy of Science and the Swedish Anthropological and Geographical Society. For the latter he edited the Geografiska Annalen for several years and many Swedish publications on agricultural meteorology founbd their way in the geography journal.
Wallén was also an honorary member of the Royal Meteorological Society, the Austrian Meteorological Society and the Royal Geographical Society.
Wallén was a prominent representative of Sweden in international scientific circles. Not only was he a member of the International Meteorological Organization since 1919, but he became also the president of the meteorological association of the Union of Geodesy and Geophysics in 1930.
